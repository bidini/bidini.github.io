Benjamin Idini

I am a graduate student in Planetary Geophysics at Caltech working with Professor David Stevenson. My broad scientific interests involve geophysical phenomena on Earth and Jupiter. On Earth, I study the mechanics of earthquakes using numerical models and remote sensing observations. On Jupiter, I investigate the structure of the core using observations of Jupiter's tidal gravitational field obtained by NASA's Juno mission. I am originally from Chile, a land of big earthquakes and powerful telescopes.

research

Jupiter

dynamical tides · spectral collocation · gravitational fields

The interior structure of Jupiter is intimately related to our understanding of the formation of planets in the Solar system. As the Moon does on Earth, the Galilean satellites raise tides on Jupiter causing observable disturbances to the gravitational field. With Dave Stevenson, we are using the Juno mission measurements of Jupiter's gravitational field to learn more about the interior of this gas giant.

Earth

slow earthquakes · rupture mechanics · numerical simulations

Earthquakes are cracks that propagate in faults. With Jean-Paul Ampuero, we used a simple model of fault zones to study the interactions between the kinematics of earthquake propagation and the degraded elastic properties observed in fault zones. We found that damaged fault-zones can induce back-propagating rupture fronts, a complex pattern of rupture propagation that has been observed in Japan and Cascadia. Moreover, our simple fault-zone model induces a pulse-like mode of rupture propagation (Idini & Ampuero 2019).

Bayesian inversion · MCMC sampling · radar interferometry

On the observational side, I used a Bayesian framework centered on Monte Carlo Markov Chain sampling to infer the distribution of slip associated with the 2019 Ridgecrest earthquake sequence, the largest seismic event in Southern California in the last 20 years. In collaboration with other scientists and radar engineers from Caltech/JPL, we revealed the complex structure of this large event with unprecedented detail after synthetizing the analisis of large amounts of remote sensing and seismic data (Ross et al. 2019).

ground motion · Chilean seismicity · signal processing

As part of my early education in earthquake engineering, I developed an empirical quantification of the ground motion expected during large earthquakes in Chile using seismic data from regional accelerometers (Idini et al. 2016).
